The Epistemological Shift

Classical Econometrics vs. Machine Learning

Classical Econometrics

  • Goal: Recover the true DGP
  • Target: \(\mathbb{E}[Y \mid do(X)]\) (Causal)
  • Setting: \(T \gg p\) (Low-dimensional)

Focuses on unbiasedness, parameter identification, and structural policy invariance (Lucas Critique).

The Algorithmic Turn

  • Goal: Minimize OOS Loss
  • Target: \(\hat{f}(X) \approx \mathbb{E}[Y \mid X]\) (Predictive)
  • Setting: \(p \approx T\) or \(p > T\)

Focuses on generalization, variance reduction via shrinkage, and handling massive, unstructured data.

The Fourth Revolution (Villena, 2026): ML is not a rejection of econometrics; it is an evolution forced by the data explosion, trading parameter interpretability for forecast stability.

Historical Context: The Four Revolutions

Era Paradigm Key Developments
1950s Dynamic Structuralism Cowles Tradition
1970s–80s Atheoretical Turn Box-Jenkins (’70) & VARs (’80)
1980s–90s Cointegration GARCH (’82) & VECM (’87+)
2010s Algorithmic Regularization & Deep Learning

Note: This is a highly stylized heuristic timeline. Each phase emerged to solve the empirical failures of the previous paradigm (e.g., Sims’ critique leading to VARs).

The Prediction Problem & Evaluation

The Bias-Variance Tradeoff

In modern macro, OLS yields \(\text{Bias} \approx 0\), but Variance explodes as predictors \(p \to T\). ML algorithms actively introduce bias to suppress this variance.

The total expected prediction error can be decomposed as:

\[ \text{MSE}(\hat{f}) = \text{Bias}^2(\hat{f}) + \text{Var}(\hat{f}) + \sigma^2 \]

  • As model complexity increases, Bias decreases but Variance increases.
  • The optimal model minimizes total error at the regularized ML region — not at OLS.

Evaluating Time Series: Avoiding Data Leakage

Standard \(K\)-fold cross-validation fails in time series due to temporal dependence. We must use strict sequential splits to mirror real-world forecasting.

The Curse of Dimensionality & Overfitting

The Limits of Classical OLS

What breaks OLS?

Ordinary Least Squares fits a hyperplane by strictly minimizing in-sample MSE.

Vulnerabilities in Modern Macro:

  • Highly sensitive to structural breaks and outliers.
  • Cannot handle severe multicollinearity (singular \(X^\top X\)).
  • Fails completely when predictors outnumber observations (\(p \gg T\)).

The Curse of Dimensionality

As we add more variables (FRED-MD, text data), the mathematical volume of the data space grows exponentially.

Data points become extremely sparse. OLS reacts by assigning massive, unstable coefficients to “chase” the noise, destroying Out-of-Sample (OOS) predictive power.

Visualizing the Problem: Overfitting

✅ Good Fit (Learned the DGP)

The model captures the general structural trend. A simple linear fit through the data — it performs well on unseen rolling windows.

❌ Overfitting (Memorized the Noise)

Like memorizing a practice test. \(R^2 \approx 1\) in-sample, but MSE explodes out-of-sample. The model zigzags through every data point.

Why Classic Rules Break: The Failure of Distance

The “Magic Playground”

Imagine playing tag in a room with 1,000 directions to run. Because there is so much empty space, everyone runs into their own corner.

Result: Everyone is exactly 1 mile away from you. If everyone is the same distance, the concept of “closest” disappears!

When algorithms measure distance between 10,000 variables, the measurements become mathematically useless.

Regularization: Ridge and Lasso

The Solution: Regularization (Shrinkage)

To survive high-dimensional environments (\(p \gg T\)), we must enforce mathematical discipline to control bias.

Instead of just minimizing empirical error, we minimize the error PLUS a penalty for complexity:

\[ \hat{\beta} = \arg\min_{\beta} \left\{ \mathcal{L}(Y, X\beta) + \lambda \cdot \mathcal{P}(\beta) \right\} \]

  • \(\lambda \ge 0\) is the tuning parameter controlling the severity of the penalty.
  • By forcing the model to generate smaller parameters, we trade a small amount of bias for a massive reduction in variance.

Ridge Regression (\(L_2\) Regularization)

Ridge Regression adds a squared Euclidean norm penalty to the objective:

\[ \hat{\beta}_{\text{Ridge}} = \arg\min_{\beta} \left\{ \frac{1}{2T} \|Y - X\beta\|_2^2 + \lambda \sum_{j=1}^p \beta_j^2 \right\} \]

Key Properties:

  • Smooth Shrinkage: Pulls coefficients toward zero smoothly, but rarely reaches exactly zero.
  • Analytic Solution: Easily solved via linear algebra:

\[\hat{\beta}_{\text{Ridge}} = (X^\top X + \lambda I)^{-1} X^\top Y\]

  • Econometric Link: Mathematically equivalent to a Bayesian VAR (BVAR) with a Gaussian prior centered at zero (e.g., Minnesota Prior).

Lasso: Penalized vs. Constrained Formulation

The Lasso (\(L_1\)) estimator can be mathematically expressed in two equivalent forms:

1. The Penalized Form:

\[ \min_{\beta} \left\{ \frac{1}{2T} \|y - X\beta\|_2^2 + \lambda \|\beta\|_1 \right\} \]

Here, \(\lambda \geq 0\) acts as the penalty weight controlling the shrinkage intensity.

2. The Constrained Form:

\[ \min_{\beta} \left\{ \frac{1}{2T} \|y - X\beta\|_2^2 \right\} \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \|\beta\|_1 \leq t \]

Here, \(t \geq 0\) acts as an absolute “budget” for the coefficients. The KKT conditions establish that for every budget \(t\), there exists a corresponding penalty \(\lambda\).

The Geometry of Shrinkage

Why does Lasso select variables (\(\beta_j = 0\)) while Ridge does not?

Lasso (\(L_1\) Diamond)

The feasible region is a diamond shape with sharp corners. The loss function contours tend to hit exactly on a corner (axis), forcing one coefficient to zero.

The sharp corners mean the contour hits exactly on an axis (\(\beta_1 = 0\)).

Ridge (\(L_2\) Circle)

The feasible region is a smooth circle. The loss function contours intersect the boundary off the axis, shrinking both coefficients without eliminating either.

The circular boundary intersects off the axis, shrinking both without eliminating either.

The Mathematical Logic of the Penalties

Let’s look at a numerical example to see how the penalties behave differently.

Suppose our current coefficients are \(\beta = [10, 5]\). What happens if the optimizer reduces the first parameter by exactly 1 unit (to 9)?

Scenario Ridge (\(L_2\)) Penalty \(\left(\sum \beta_i^2\right)\) Lasso (\(L_1\)) Penalty \(\left(\sum |\beta_i|\right)\)
Initial: \([10, 5]\) \(10^2 + 5^2 = 125\) \(10 + 5 = 15\)
New: \([9, 5]\) \(9^2 + 5^2 = 106\) \(9 + 5 = 14\)
Reduction Size −19 −1

Insight: Ridge prioritizes shrinking large parameters because squaring large numbers increases the penalty exponentially. Lasso treats all unit reductions equally, allowing it to push a parameter to exactly zero.

Summary Comparison

Feature Ridge (\(L_2\)) Lasso (\(L_1\))
Primary Goal Handle multicollinearity & stabilize Feature selection & simplicity
Resulting Model All features included (small weights) Sparse model (irrelevant features = 0)
Computational Solution Closed-form (Matrix algebra) Numerical optimization (Iterative)
Bias-Variance Tradeoff Lower variance, slightly higher bias Lower variance, potentially higher bias

Both models are essential tools for modern econometricians navigating data-rich (\(p \gg T\)) environments!

Lecture 1 Mini-Lab: Leakage-Safe Shrinkage

The Project Task: Build a leakage-safe Ridge/Lasso forecast for US YoY CPI (our canonical target) and compare it to a classical OLS benchmark using provided starter code.

Two R Scripts are provided for this lab:

  1. Script 1 (Baseline): A static train-test split to introduce the glmnet syntax and AR(\(p\)) feature engineering.
  2. Script 2 (The Protocol): A rigorous \(h\)-step ahead direct forecasting loop using rolling windows across multiple horizons.

Understanding Script 1: The Baseline AR(12) Pipeline

Core Mechanics of the Script:

  • Feature Construction: We use embed(inflation, p + 1) to automatically construct the \(\mathbf{X}\) matrix of \(t-1 \dots t-12\) lags without manual looping.
  • Temporal Split: We strictly split the data at the 80% mark (1:split_index). No random sampling is used to prevent future information leaking into the training set.
  • Tuning \(\lambda\): The function cv.glmnet(x_train, y_train) performs \(k\)-fold cross-validation to find lambda.min.
  • ⚠️ Important Note: We use standard \(k\)-fold here only to introduce the syntax. Because random \(k\)-fold breaks temporal order, all substantive results must use rolling/blocked CV or a time-ordered validation scheme!

Understanding Script 2: Rolling Windows & Direct Forecast

The second script implements our Seminar Project evaluation protocol:

1. Direct \(h\)-step Ahead Forecasting:

Instead of forecasting \(t+1\) and iterating to \(t+h\), we model \(y_{t+h}\) directly as a function of \(X_t\). The script shifts the feature index back by \(h\):

train_feature_idx <- train_target_idx - h

2. The Rolling Loop:

  • Outer Loops: Iterate over forecast horizons (\(h = 1, 3, 6, 12\)) and window sizes (\(T = 60, 120\) months).
  • Inner Loop (step in 1:OOS_steps): Moves the training window forward exactly one month at a time, retraining the OLS, Ridge, and Lasso models at every single step before predicting exactly one out-of-sample data point.

Why did OLS win? In a low-dimensional setting where the model is well-specified and variance is already moderate, shrinkage can add bias without providing enough variance reduction to improve OOS RMSE.

Lab Results: Rolling Windows & Long Horizons

When Machine Learning saves the forecast:

  • Long Horizons (\(h=12\)): Signal-to-noise ratio drops. OLS overfits to noise, causing RMSE to explode.
  • Small Windows (\(T=60\)): With only 5 observations per parameter, OLS variance becomes unstable.
  • The ML Edge: Ridge and Lasso act as “insurance.” By penalizing large coefficients, they stabilize the model during volatile periods or when degrees of freedom are scarce.

Takeaway: Shrinkage is a requirement for stability, not just an option.

Is Inflation “Linear” and “Stationary”? Implications for Forecasting and Shrinkage

Key point: Treating YoY CPI inflation as strictly linear and stationary is often a useful local approximation, but it is not a safe global assumption for forecasting or policy inference.

1) Stationarity: what is plausibly true.

  • Monthly CPI levels are nonstationary; YoY inflation is more stable and can look stationary in long samples.
  • Still, inflation exhibits persistent dynamics, time-varying volatility and structural breaks (policy regimes, supply shocks, measurement/revisions).

2) Linearity: what is plausibly true.

  • An AR(12) is a linear conditional-mean model; it can fit well when dynamics are stable.
  • Nonlinearities can arise from regime dependence (high vs low inflation), asymmetric responses to shocks, and changing persistence.

3) Why OLS may beat Ridge/Lasso in your AR(12).

  • With only \(p=12\) regressors and decent \(T\), OLS variance is already low; shrinkage adds bias and may not reduce RMSE.
  • If \(\lambda\) is tuned with random \(k\)-fold CV, time-order is violated and results can be distorted; use rolling/blocked tuning.

4) What to do in practice (seminar rule).

  • Diagnose: unit-root tests + break tests; check stability with rolling estimates; examine residual autocorrelation and heteroskedasticity.
  • Evaluate honestly: rolling/expanding OOS, multi-horizon loss (RMSE/MAE), and (when relevant) forecast comparison tests.
  • Expect ML gains mainly in data-rich, unstable, or nonlinear environments (many predictors, regime shifts, interactions).

Concluding Remarks

Project: 10-Minute Check-In

Our Standard Closing Questions: 1. What changed in OOS RMSE by horizon? 2. Which failure mode did we fix (or create)?

Today’s Answers:

  • RMSE Shift: OLS dominated \(h=1\) and large windows (\(T=120\)). ML Shrinkage drastically improved \(h=12\) on small windows (\(T=60\)).
  • Failure Mode Fixed: We fixed the variance explosion of OLS in data-scarce, long-horizon settings.
  • Failure Mode Created: We introduced bias. In highly stable, low-dimensional settings, Ridge/Lasso mathematically underperformed OLS.

-   **Inner Loop (`step in 1:OOS_steps`):** Moves the training window forward exactly one month at a time, retraining the OLS, Ridge, and Lasso models at every single step before predicting exactly one out-of-sample data point.

### Is Inflation "Linear" and "Stationary"? Implications for Forecasting and Shrinkage

**Key point:** Treating YoY CPI inflation as strictly linear and stationary is often a useful *local approximation*, but it is not a safe global assumption for forecasting or policy inference.

**1) Stationarity: what is plausibly true.**

-   Monthly CPI levels are nonstationary; YoY inflation is *more* stable and can look stationary in long samples.
-   Still, inflation exhibits **persistent dynamics**, **time-varying volatility** and **structural breaks** (policy regimes, supply shocks, measurement/revisions).

**2) Linearity: what is plausibly true.**

-   An AR(12) is a linear conditional-mean model; it can fit well when dynamics are stable.
-   Nonlinearities can arise from **regime dependence** (high vs low inflation), **asymmetric responses** to shocks, and **changing persistence**.

**3) Why OLS may beat Ridge/Lasso in your AR(12).**

-   With only $p=12$ regressors and decent $T$, OLS variance is already low; shrinkage adds bias and may not reduce RMSE.
-   If $\lambda$ is tuned with random $k$-fold CV, time-order is violated and results can be distorted; use rolling/blocked tuning.

**4) What to do in practice (seminar rule).**

-   Diagnose: unit-root tests + break tests; check stability with rolling estimates; examine residual autocorrelation and heteroskedasticity.
-   Evaluate honestly: rolling/expanding OOS, multi-horizon loss (RMSE/MAE), and (when relevant) forecast comparison tests.
-   Expect ML gains mainly in **data-rich**, **unstable**, or **nonlinear** environments (many predictors, regime shifts, interactions).
